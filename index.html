How San Francisco crime composition shifted after 2018

Looking at San Francisco incident reports as one total hides an important change. Around 2018, the composition of incidents begins to shift, and after 2020 that split widens further: non-personal crime stays elevated, while personal crime settles into a lower range.

By Ylfa · DTU Social Data Analysis & Visualization

It is tempting to talk about crime as if it moves in one direction for the whole city. This dataset suggests something less simple. When I group incidents into personal crime and non-personal crime, a clear split starts to appear around 2018.

I define personal crime here as assault and robbery. I define non-personal crime as vehicle theft and burglary. This is not meant to be a perfect legal classification. It is a simple way to compare incidents that involve direct interpersonal harm with incidents that are more tied to property and opportunity.

Important caveat: police incident data is not a perfect measure of underlying crime. It reflects reporting, recording, enforcement, and administrative systems as well as behavior itself. The story below should therefore be read as a story about reported incidents, not a complete measure of harm.

A visible split starts around 2018

The first figure tracks both groups over time and indexes them to the same starting point. That makes it easier to compare change in pattern rather than raw volume. The key point is not that one line is always larger. The key point is that the two groups stop moving together.

Around 2018, both groups spike and then fall, but they do not settle in the same place. After that reset, non-personal crime remains structurally above its earlier level, while personal crime drops below its older range. The period after 2020 widens that gap even more.

This is why I think the most useful summary is not that crime simply rose or fell. A better summary is that the mix of reported incidents changed. The next question is whether that shift is evenly spread across San Francisco or concentrated in particular places.

The non-personal side of the shift is spatially uneven

To make the geographic part concrete, I focus on vehicle theft, one of the clearest non-personal categories in the dataset. The map below includes incidents from 2018 onward. What stands out is that the post-2018 pattern is not evenly spread across the city.

Instead, vehicle theft clusters in specific areas. That matters because it suggests that the composition shift is not only about time. It also has a spatial structure. Some parts of the city contribute much more than others to the rise in non-personal incidents.

The shift also looks different across districts and categories

The final figure compares district-category combinations before and after the 2018 shift. Each point is one district paired with one of the four categories used in the story. Points above the diagonal became more common after 2018, while points below became less common.

This view matters because it moves beyond the citywide average. It shows which local combinations sit underneath the broader change in composition. If many non-personal points are above the diagonal and many personal points are below it, then the split in Figure 1 is not just a visual impression. It is being produced by multiple local changes at once.

Taken together, the three figures suggest that San Francisco did not simply experience more or less crime after 2018. Instead, the composition of reported incidents changed, and that change was carried by specific categories and specific parts of the city.
