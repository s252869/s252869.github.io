San Francisco Crime Analysis

This website presents a small set of visualizations exploring reported crime in San Francisco. The project looks at how total crime changes over time, how selected crime categories develop across years, and where incidents tend to cluster geographically across the city.

Start with the overview Jump to visualizations

Overview

The goal of this project is to move beyond raw counts and use visualization to understand larger patterns in the data. Together, the charts and map answer three main questions: how crime changes over time, which categories dominate, and where incidents are concentrated.

Main takeaway: reported crime appears relatively stable for many years, peaks around 2018, and then declines noticeably after 2020. At the same time, theft remains the dominant category, while the map shows that incidents cluster in dense and highly active urban areas.

What is happening in the yearly trend?

The yearly line chart shows that reported crime stays within a fairly similar range for much of the period from 2003 to around 2017. There is then a visible peak around 2018, followed by a strong decline beginning around 2019–2020.

  • 2003–2017: crime is relatively stable, with moderate ups and downs rather than dramatic shifts.
  • 2018 peak: the dataset reaches its highest level, suggesting a short period of increased reported activity.
  • Post-2020 decline: reported crime drops sharply, which may reflect major changes in city activity and mobility during and after COVID.
  • 2026 value: the final year is extremely low and should be interpreted carefully, because it is likely incomplete rather than a true annual total.

This is a good example of why context matters: a sharp drop at the end of a series does not always mean crime truly collapsed. It may also mean that the latest year contains only partial data.

Category-level trends

The interactive line chart makes it easier to compare how different crime categories evolve. Theft stands out as the largest category over time, while assault, burglary, robbery, and vehicle theft follow much lower trajectories.

  • Theft dominates: it remains the largest category throughout the period and drives much of the total volume.
  • Different patterns by crime type: not all categories move in the same way, which shows why looking only at the total can hide important detail.
  • Interactive value: hover labels and category toggling make it easier to isolate specific trends and compare categories directly.

Spatial concentration of crime

The map shows the geographic spread of sampled incidents across San Francisco. Rather than being evenly distributed, crime appears to cluster in central and dense urban areas.

  • Visible clustering: incidents are concentrated rather than randomly spread across the city.
  • Urban intensity matters: central districts with more activity, movement, and density appear to contain more incidents.
  • Map exploration: zooming and panning make it possible to inspect local patterns that are impossible to see in a simple summary table.

This map uses sampled incidents, so it is best read as a picture of spatial pattern rather than an exact count comparison between neighborhoods.

Reflection

Building this site made it clear that publishing a visualization is not just about exporting a chart. It also requires structure, context, and explanation. The combination of a summary figure, an interactive category chart, and a map creates a more complete view of crime patterns than any one figure alone.

For the next stage, this website can be developed into a more narrative data story by focusing on one especially interesting finding, such as the post-2020 decline, the dominance of theft, or the concentration of incidents in central districts.
