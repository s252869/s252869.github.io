How San Francisco crime composition shifted after 2018

Looking at San Francisco incident reports as a single total hides something important. Around 2018, the mix of reported incidents begins to split: non-personal crime surges while personal crime falls. The city did not simply get more or less dangerous — it got different.

By Ylfa Margret & Tanja Kristin · DTU Social Data Analysis & Visualization

The San Francisco Police Department publishes incident reports going back to 2003. Each record is a single reported incident — a burglary, an assault, a vehicle theft — along with a date, a location, and the police district where it was filed. The dataset covers millions of incidents and is publicly available.

For this story we focus on four categories: assault and robbery, grouped as personal crime because they involve direct harm to a person, and vehicle theft and burglary, grouped as non-personal crime because the harm is primarily to property. This is a deliberate simplification designed to make one specific pattern visible — not a legal classification.

A caveat worth reading first. Police incident data measures what gets reported and recorded, not crime itself. A drop in arrests can reflect a change in enforcement priorities just as easily as a change in behavior. A spike in vehicle theft reports can reflect a real wave of theft, a shift in how the department codes incidents, or both. The patterns below are patterns in reported incidents — keep that distinction in mind.

A visible split starts around 2018

Before 2018 the two groups move together — rising and falling in roughly the same rhythm across more than a decade. Then around mid-2018 both series spike sharply. What matters is where they land afterward.

Non-personal crime rebounds strongly and stays elevated well above its pre-2018 level through the early 2020s before declining again. Personal crime does the opposite: it falls below its historical range and stays there. The COVID period deepens both movements temporarily, but the structural gap was already opening before the pandemic arrived.

The most honest summary is not that crime rose or fell. It is that the composition of reported incidents changed. That raises a geographic question: did this happen everywhere in the city, or was it concentrated in specific places?

The increase was concentrated in a handful of districts

If the post-2018 shift were uniform across the city, every police district would show a similar increase. The map tells a different story. The Tenderloin — a small, densely policed district near the center of San Francisco — stands out immediately with an increase of over 100%. Most other districts also increased, but by considerably less.

This uneven geography matters. It suggests the citywide trend in Figure 1 is being pulled upward by a small number of specific places, not by a broad city-wide change. Hover over any district to see the exact percentage.

A large percentage increase in the Tenderloin does not by itself prove that more crime is occurring there relative to elsewhere — it could also reflect more reporting, denser officer presence, or changes in how incidents are classified. But it does identify where in the data the shift is most visible, and that points the story toward a specific question: is the Tenderloin genuinely different from the rest of the city, or is it just an extreme version of a city-wide pattern?

Tenderloin is an outlier — but no district was untouched

The final figure compares Tenderloin directly to every other district, one at a time, across all four crime categories. Use the dropdown to select any district and see how it compares. The two dots on each row show the percentage change in that category: navy for Tenderloin, orange for the selected district.

The vehicle theft row is the most striking. Against almost every comparison district, Tenderloin's increase is dramatically larger — the gap between the two dots is wide, and Tenderloin's dot sits far to the right. Burglary shows a similar but smaller gap. Assault and robbery, by contrast, show the two dots clustered close together, sometimes with the comparison district even slightly higher than Tenderloin.

Taken together, the three figures tell a coherent story. San Francisco's crime composition shifted after 2018, with non-personal crime rising and personal crime falling. That shift was geographically uneven, concentrated especially in the Tenderloin. And within that shift, vehicle theft is the category that most distinguishes the Tenderloin from the rest of the city — while personal crime moved in similar ways almost everywhere.

What drove the Tenderloin's vehicle theft increase specifically is a question this data cannot fully answer. The numbers reflect reported incidents, and reported incidents reflect policing decisions, enforcement priorities, and reporting behavior as much as they reflect actual crime. But the pattern is consistent enough — and distinct enough — to be worth taking seriously.
